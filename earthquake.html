EARTHQUAKE PREPARATION



An earthquake is the shaking of the Earth's surface resulting from a sudden release of energy in the Earth's lithosphere that creates seismic waves. Earthquakes can:
  • Cause severe damage to buildings, bridges, and infrastructure.
  • Lead to landslides, tsunamis, and liquefaction.
  • Occur anywhere in the world, but are most common along tectonic plate boundaries.

IF YOU ARE UNDER AN EARTHQUAKE WARNING:

  • Drop, Cover, and Hold On! Get down on your hands and knees. Cover your head and neck with your arms or take cover under a sturdy piece of furniture. Hold On until the shaking stops.
  • Stay indoors if you are already inside. Move away from windows, glass, and heavy furniture that may fall.
  • If you are outside, move to an open area away from buildings, trees, streetlights, and utility wires.
  • If you are in a vehicle, pull over to a clear area and stay inside with your seatbelt fastened until the shaking stops.

HOW TO STAY SAFE WHEN AN EARTHQUAKE THREATENS

Prepare NOW

  • Learn how to secure heavy furniture and appliances to the walls to prevent tipping.
  • Identify safe spots in each room, such as under sturdy tables or against inside walls.
  • Develop a family emergency plan that includes meeting places and communication methods.
  • Have an emergency kit prepared with food, water, medical supplies, and important documents.
  • Sign up for your communityâ€™s alert system for earthquake warnings.

Survive DURING

  • If you are indoors, stay there. Drop, Cover, and Hold On until the shaking stops.
  • If you are outdoors, move to an open area away from buildings, trees, and utility wires.
  • If you are in a vehicle, stop in a clear area and stay inside until the shaking stops.

Be Safe AFTER

  • Check yourself and others for injuries and provide first aid as necessary.
  • Listen to local news and authorities for information about safety and emergency assistance.
  • Expect aftershocks. They can happen minutes, days, or even weeks after the initial quake.
  • Be cautious when entering damaged buildings and be aware of potential hazards.
  • Document property damage with photographs for insurance claims.
  • Use text messages or social media to communicate with family and friends to avoid overloading phone systems.
